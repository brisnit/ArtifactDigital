Late in 2020, I was up in LA at a Reggie Watts listening party. My friend texted me about some new AI that was a game changer. It was OpenAI. I immediately jumped on it. I felt like my brain was going to explode. The speed at which I was working was incredible.

About a month later, we got an email from IT at work: “Don’t touch AI until we figure out if it’s safe or not.” Sorry, I was already a cyborg. I learned some things early on that have been very helpful as AI has risen from a tech buzzword to something the entire world is awarding the “Best Thing Since Sliced Bread” title.

Early on in my entanglement with AI, I asked it a fun question: “What should I be doing so that I don’t lose my job to you?” Honestly, I already knew the answer, but what it shot back to me was vital.

Chat started with an important truth: there are things it can do tremendously better than me, but there are things I can do that it can’t do at all. AI is otherworldly at generating ideas, drafting frameworks, producing first-pass artifacts, and speeding up thinking. I had other powers that, when combined with AI, could make me unstoppable. I can own outcomes, navigate politics, build trust, make judgment calls under ambiguity, read rooms, clients, and teams, and remain accountable when something fails.

I realized that AI could quickly generate options. At first, back in 2020, those options were pretty rough. But I still had the power to make decisions based on what was presented. I still needed to set priorities under constraints and make tradeoffs visible to clients. I still had all the same deliverables. I was just able to do them faster.

I also learned that sometimes, even if you arrive at the correct solution very quickly, it’s wise to sit on it for a moment before sharing, allowing insights to fully integrate into your own vocabulary. I still had to frame the problem correctly. I still held control over the narrative and over achieving alignment internally and with clients.

Design leadership is not about being right. It’s about being appropriate. We bring nuance to the table, something AI simply cannot do. AI is a tool, with many permutations, and if we are wise, we will harness its power rather than fear it. We should be leaning all the way in to as many of these tools as possible.

To achieve longevity in any field, we must be ever-learners. We must remain hungry and not allow the mundanity of life to push us into fragmented rooms of mental isolation. Acting from a place of curiosity and passion, we can use AI as a force multiplier. Because of AI, the force of our research and the force of our organization have been multiplied many times over.

AI is an excellent thought partner. We can ask it questions and explore outcomes that may have been unseen before. We can generate drafts that kickstart conversations among teams and clients. Gone are the days of procrastination driven by fear of “how to start.”

Chat concluded with an uncomfortable but empowering truth: “If someone loses their job to AI, it’s rarely because the technology is better at their role, but because they stayed focused on execution instead of evolving into leadership, mistaking volume of output for real impact.”

As an Experience Design Director, or really at any stage of a career, your advantage comes from setting direction, making informed decisions, building trust across teams and stakeholders, developing people, and owning outcomes end to end. When you operate at this level, AI isn’t a competitor. It’s a tool that supports your leadership rather than replaces it.
