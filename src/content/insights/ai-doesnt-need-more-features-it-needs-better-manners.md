*We confused adding AI with designing AI. They're very different things.*

If you've used AI in the last year, you've probably had the same experience I have.

You open your favorite design tool and suddenly there's an AI button.

Open your email? AI.

Open your presentation software? AI.

Your calendar wants to summarize your meetings. Your notes app wants to rewrite your thoughts. Your browser wants to answer questions you didn't ask.

It's like every product team on Earth got together and decided the solution to every problem was, "What if we put a sparkle icon here?"

Don't get me wrong — I love AI.

I use it every day.

But somewhere along the way, we confused *adding* AI with *designing* AI.

Those are very different things.

## We've Been Solving the Wrong Problem

For the last decade, designers have obsessed over reducing clicks.

Can we make this flow three steps instead of four? Can we move this button? Can we flatten the navigation?

Those are worthwhile questions.

But AI changes the equation.

The question isn't *"How do we make this task faster?"*

It's *"Should this person even be doing this task in the first place?"*

That's a wildly different design challenge.

And honestly — it's a much more interesting one.

## AI Should Feel Like Backup

One of my favorite people to work with is the teammate who quietly removes problems before I even notice them.

They don't announce themselves. They don't interrupt me every ten minutes. They don't ask if I'd like help.

They just… help.

That's exactly how AI should feel.

Not like a supervisor. Not like a robot trying to impress me. Not like a pop-up asking if I want it to "enhance" something.

It should feel like someone clearing the runway so I can actually do my job.

Because the work that matters isn't resizing images or writing placeholder copy.

It's deciding what deserves to exist. It's reading a room. It's understanding people. It's making judgment calls.

No model is replacing that.

## Designers Need to Stop Designing for AI

This one usually gets me in trouble.

I don't think we should be designing products around AI.

I think we should design products around humans… that happen to contain AI.

There's a difference.

Too many experiences today feel like they were built to show off what the model can do instead of helping someone accomplish what they came to accomplish.

That's backwards.

People don't wake up hoping to use AI today.

They wake up hoping to launch a business… finish a proposal… hire someone… sell a product… or spend dinner with their family instead of answering email.

AI is just one ingredient in helping them get there.

Not the destination.

## Invisible Design Is the Goal

The best interfaces I've ever used all have one thing in common.

I stop noticing them.

That's true of good architecture. Good typography. Good interaction design. Good branding.

And I think it'll be true of AI.

The best AI products five years from now probably won't advertise themselves as AI products.

You'll just think… *"Wow, that was surprisingly easy."*

That's the win.

## At Artifact, We Prototype Before We Debate

One thing I love about our team is that we don't spend weeks arguing over ideas.

We build them. Fast.

Not because speed is trendy. Because reality is honest.

Everyone has opinions until you put something in front of a customer.

That's why AI has changed our workflow so dramatically.

Instead of discussing a feature for two weeks, we can often prototype it in a day. Sometimes in an afternoon.

Then we watch someone use it.

That moment tells us more than twenty meetings ever could.

The prototype isn't the product. It's the conversation starter.

## Good Design Is Becoming Better Judgment

I think the role of designers is changing.

Not disappearing. Changing.

There was a time when knowing every Photoshop shortcut made you valuable. Then it became knowing responsive design. Then design systems.

Now? It's judgment.

Knowing what should exist. Knowing what doesn't need to exist. Knowing when to automate something. Knowing when not to.

Anyone can generate ten layouts now.

Choosing the right one — that's still design.

## More Features Rarely Win

I've worked with brands that serve millions of people.

One lesson keeps repeating itself.

Customers almost never ask for more features. They ask for less friction.

Those aren't the same thing.

Sometimes the best feature you can build is removing three others.

AI gives us an incredible opportunity to simplify products instead of making them more complicated.

I hope we take it.

## The Real Opportunity

I think we're entering one of the most exciting periods our industry has ever seen.

Not because AI is replacing designers.

Because it's removing the work that never made us designers in the first place.

The repetitive production. The busywork. The endless formatting. The tasks we tolerated because computers couldn't help us before.

Now they can.

Which means we get to spend more time doing what people actually hire designers for.

Thinking. Questioning. Connecting ideas. Understanding people. Creating experiences that feel obvious in hindsight.

That's the fun part.

## My Hope

My hope isn't that every product becomes "AI-powered."

My hope is that people stop talking about AI entirely.

Because when it's truly designed well… it disappears.

The technology fades into the background. The human comes into focus.

And the product simply feels… thoughtful.

At Artifact Digital, that's the standard we're chasing.

Not because AI is the future.

Because people are.
